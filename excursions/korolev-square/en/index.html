ABOUT THE LOCATION

MAIDAN KOROLIOVA (KOROLIOV’S SQUARE)

The main administrative centres of the city and the region are located here. In the 17th and early 19th centuries, a small nameless river flowed through this place, so the surrounding area was swampy. In the late 18th century, this spot was the city’s borderline. To the left, there was a large garden belonging to the Monastery of the Sisters of Our Lady of Mercy. In the 1950s, the regional council building and a small square were constructed, forming what was then known as the Maidan of the City Council. In 1971, a monument to Academician Serhiy Koroliov was unveiled, and for the 100th anniversary of his birth, the square was renamed “Maidan Koroliova”.

MARIINSKY WOMEN'S GYMNASIUM

The building, originally constructed as a tenement house in the mid-19th century, was later acquired by the Mariinsky Women's Gymnasium. By the late 19th century, it was the only secondary school for women in the Volyn Governorate. Among its notable students were Zoia Gaidai, Nina and Olga Kosach, Veronika Morozivna, and the mother of Sviatoslav Richter. After the gymnasium was closed in 1919, the building was repurposed as a first aid station, a school, and the Korolov District Executive Committee, and, since 1983, it has housed the Zhytomyr Center for Children's and Youth Arts and Creativity.

ARCHBISHOP'S COMPOUND

Established in 1840 on land belonging to the Sisters of Our Lady of Mercy, the compound served as the residence of the diocesan bishop. Taras Shevchenko visited it in 1846. The compound also housed the editorial office of Volyn newspaper, where Mykhailo Kotsiubynsky worked, as well as the editorial office of the newspaper Borot’ba (in English: The Struggle) in 1919. For a time, the Ukrainian Autocephalous Orthodox Church, led by priest Mykola Khomychevsky, operated within the complex.

ZHYTOMYR FAIRS

In the second half of the 19th century, Zhytomyr became the centre of trade in the Volyn area. Three ten-day fairs were held annually, with the largest on Sinna Square. Cloths, furs, salt, and fish were sold there, and a hop fair was held on Khlibna Street. The Malovanskyi Fair specialised in the horse and cattle trade. Later, other marketplaces appeared in the city, including the Khlibnyi and Khmilnyky markets.

WHO HONOURED IT?

YEVHEN KONTSEVYCH

Born in 1935 in Mlynyshche, Yevhen Kontsevych was a writer who turned hardship into creativity. At 17, a failed dive left him bedridden, but he found solace in literature.

His writing debut came in 1956, and in 1962, literary critic Leonid Novychenko praised his deep humanism. Kontsevych’s works, marked by integrity, rejected hypocrisy and injustice.

In 1972, his support for the dissident movement led to a KGB search and a publishing ban. Only in 1985 was his second book, Leaving the Cheekbone. His novel Live, Live, Live! embodied his life philosophy: “I can fight.I will strain my will—only to live.”

Despite challenges, Kontsevych kept his humour, jokingly calling himself an “honorary citizen of Zhytomyr” with free tram rides—even abroad. A fighter to the end, he inspired perseverance against all odds.

SEVEN-METRE HOMESPUN TOWEL BY YEVHEN KONTSEVYCH

One of the unique symbols of his life is a seven-meter-long homespun towel on which famous Ukrainian figures, including Borys Ten, Viacheslav Chornovil, Ivan Dziuba, Yevhen Hutsalo, and others, left their signatures for 50 years. Kontsevych embroidered the autographs, and his wife was involved in the process. After the writer's death in July 2010, the towel was transferred to the Zhytomyr Literary Museum. In 2023, the towel was shown to visitors for the first time during the literary art zone “Mosaic of the Ukrainian Soul” in the Zhytomyr Regional Library for Children and Youth.

ABOUT THE WORK “ЛОЖКИ” (“SPOONS”)

“Spoons” is an underrated novella by Yevhen Kontsevych, offering deep insight into his inner world. Through the protagonist, Andriy, the story reflects the author’s struggles, emotions, and search for meaning. Interestingly, Kontsevych also carved spoons in real life to earn a living.

At first glance, the novella seems simple, yet it brims with intense emotions—loss of hope, frustration, and despair. Andriy’s pain mirrors Kontsevych’s own: “He asked her for such a pittance… his throat was peppered with an acrid aftertaste he could neither swallow nor exhale.”

The story explores resilience and faith, emphasizing that hardships are trials meant to strengthen the soul. The ending reveals life’s true meaning—cherishing what we have before it’s lost.
