THE WORK

EXCERPT FROM “АЛМАЗНЕ ЖОРНО” (DIAMOND MILLSTON)

“

* Всі події твору, окрім ІІ дії, відбуваються в Житомирі 1768-1769рр.

Дія третя

В домі Авраама Цікловіца в Житомирі. Праворуч стіл, покритий до самої землі оксамитовим килимом: на ньому високий канделябр і велика книга з срібними застіжками на різьбленому пюпітрі. Великий глобус з мідним екватором на точених стовпчиках. Велика підзорна труба, килими, шафи з книжками. Просто проти сцени широкий східний ослін, укритий килимом і оксамитовими подушками. Двері — праворуч вхідні, ліворуч — в інші кімнати.

Дія п'ята

Досить широке місце перед брамою, що веде на Кафедральний плац у Житомирі. За широкою брамою з архітектурними оздобами і статуями над просвітом видно плац, католицький собор і народ, що сновигає по плацу. Обік просвіту брами дві лучкові ніші, що правлять за приміщення для варти. Там на порозі на табуретках сидить кілька жовнірів у мундирах і білих гамашах з люльками в зубах. Ліворуч біля самої брами — шинок під вивіскою “Золотий млин” з 2-3 столиками коло входу. Трохи далі одкривається широкий краєвид на поля і далекі гаї. Праворуч — ряд крамниць, зараз зачинених. Коло крайньої крамниці на землі навалено кілька чималих млинових жорен, одно на одному, а найбільше стоїть притуленим до стіни.

”

ABOUT THE LOCATION

SOBORNA SQUARE AND THE PHARMACY

In the second half of the 18th century, the territory of the modern Soborna Square was part of the Bernardine Monastery. The building with pharmacy No. 127 used to stand on the site of another one, which housed the oldest pharmacy in Zhytomyr, “Старина и роскошь” (in English: “Antiquity and Luxury”). Its owner was a quarantine doctor, Franciszek Heinch. According to Volodymyr Yershov, he revived the Masonic Lodge of Scattered Darkness. His son, Karol August Heinch, became a famous playwright. Thanks to Karol, lyrics of the Ukrainian anthem "Ukraine Has Not Yet Perished..." became widespread: in 1864, the song was performed in his play The Return of the Cossacks from Trapezund on the stage of a Lviv theatre.

Zhytomyr was liberated from German troops on December 31, 1943, but the buildings around Cathedral Square were destroyed. In 1957, the former Regional Party Committee building (now home to the Main Department of Justice in Zhytomyr Region) was constructed and a 50-metre flagpole flying the flag of Ukraine was erected in the square.

A "window display exhibition" was created in the nine-story building that houses pharmacy Nº127, featuring around 20 exhibits from its storage, some over 40 years old. The pharmacy opened in 1961, has retained its original appearance. The exhibits were initially intended for a separate room but were ultimately placed in the pharmacy's display cases.

WHO HONOURED IT?

IVAN KOCHERHA

Ivan Kocherha (1881–1952) was a Ukrainian playwright, writer, and publicist whose works reflected Ukraine’s dynamic historical epochs.

Born in Nosivka, Chernihiv region, into a railway worker’s family, he grew up in a loving environment. After graduating from high school in Chernihiv (1899), he studied law at St. Volodymyr’s University in Kyiv but never practised.

His dramas, including The Diamond Millstone, gained acclaim, and Zhytomyr’s academic theatre now bears his name. His greatest work, Yaroslav the Wise (1944), won the USSR Prize and was staged by the Taras Shevchenko Drama Theatre in Kharkiv.

Fluent in several languages, Kocherha navigated periods of censorship through translations. He died in Kyiv in 1952, leaving an influential legacy in Ukrainian literature and theatre.

ABOUT THE DRAMA “АЛМАЗНЕ ЖОРНО” (DIAMOND MILLSTONE)

The drama Diamond Millstone by Ivan Kocherha was written in Zhytomyr and is dedicated to the events following the suppression of the Koliivshchyna uprising. The author chose to depict the epilogue of the movement — the nobility's brutal retaliation against the Haidamaky (Ukrainian Cossack rebels.)— focusing on the grim atmosphere of this historical period. The play is set in Zhytomyr in 1768–1769.

The plot blends intense drama with scenes of everyday life. At its core is the search for a stolen diamond belonging to Princess Vilkomirska. The main characters, Stasia and her fiancé, the Haidamak, Vasyl Khmarnyi, become entangled in a tragic chain of events. Count Ruzhensky deceitfully takes possession of the diamond and imprisons Stasia, driving her to madness. Vasyl avenges her by killing the count with a millstone. The final scenes unfold in the town square.

The image of the diamond millstone in the drama is deeply symbolic: for Stasia, it represents happiness; for the princess and the count, power; for the Jewish sage, human dignity; and for Vasyl, a tool of vengeance. As the author himself noted, the central idea of the drama is the contrast between two stones—the nobleman's worthless diamond and the simple millstone, which symbolizes both labour and the people's retribution.
