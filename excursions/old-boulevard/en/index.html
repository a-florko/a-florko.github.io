THE WORK

EXCERPTS FROM "BILETSKI"

“

That day, when Aunt Apollinaria left them, the first snow fell. Myroslava was coming back from school, as usual, through the boulevard and the park. Snowflakes began flying as she turned onto the first boulevard; it seemed to her that the trees above her head rustled, sighed, or lightly groaned. She looked up at the sky – it was lying on top of the trees, dirty blue, viscous, as if the twigs were cutting into Its swollen flesh.

The wind died down when she stepped onto the second boulevard, and it became eerily quiet. There was not a single soul on the boulevard, only herself, and frightened by that strange silence, the girl suddenly stopped. Then the sky above her head shuddered, and the snow fell raggedly, slowly; the surrounding air turned white, and snowflakes gently descended onto the yellow alley with the remains of fallen leaves, − on those leaves, on the benches, on her hand holding the backpack, on the white woollen headscarf, melting on her cheeks and forehead. She held out her palm in front of her, and snowflakes began to settle on it. Pale figures, they glowed just for a moment and disappeared, their life was like a breath.

She walked slowly, twisting a leaf, and the trees swayed like decorations shaken by a draft; the world seemed to expand in front of her – a sea of white dancing specks.

And she floated on it, or rather, walked on the bottom of the sea – a small, wispy person of this world, like one of these trees, which suddenly pulled out its roots from the ground and walked. And it seemed to her that time does not exist, that neither yesterday nor tomorrow exists, but there is only this white snowfall – the time of some great, secret revelation. It seemed to her that from the moment she set foot on this yellow earthy boulevard, between these black trunks, under these branches melted into the sky slush, she began to experience something special, unprecedented – she was leaving behind not a string of invisible traces, but herself as a child, who, walking along on this yellow road, enters not just into space, but the unexplored version of herself, into almost grown herself, the wise herself. That's why she walked so carefully, that's why she felt such quiet joy – only special moments bring such experiences.

”

ABOUT THE LOCATION

THE OLD BOULEVARD

The Old Boulevard received its current name in 1991, evolving from names like Third International Street, Stalin Prospect, and October Boulevard. It was established in the 1870s on land previously owned by the Catholic Convent of the Sisters of Mercy. In the 1960s, colonnades, descents to the Teteriv River, and residential buildings were added. By 1980, renovations for the Olympics gave the park its modern appearance. The Boulevard is divided into four parts, each with its own unique story.

The First Boulevard features remnants of a monument removed in 2022, symbolizing Ukraine’s break with its imperial Russian past. The Second Boulevard had a similar fate, with a memorial to Shchors destroyed during World War II. The Third Boulevard houses the Polissya Stadium, originally built as Dynamo but rebuilt after wartime destruction. The Fourth Boulevard honors Baron Ivan de Chaudoir, an artist, collector, and philanthropist who contributed to the area’s development. In 2024, a memorial plaque was dedicated to him, and the park was renamed in his honor.

WHO HONORED IT?

VALERIY SHEVCHUK

A Ukrainian writer-sixtier, patriarch of the Zhytomyr Prose School, and master of psychological and Gothic prose. A member of the National Writers’ Union of Ukraine, he devoted his works to preserving Ukrainian identity.

Born in a shoemaker’s family in 1939, Shevchuk grew up immersed in Ukrainian classics, sparking his lifelong passion for literature. His literary journey began with poetry, but soon shifted to prose. In 1961, he published his debut short story, “Nastunka”, dedicated to Taras Shevchenko. After studying at the Faculty of History and Philosophy in Kyiv, he joined literary studios and established himself as a prominent writer by the 1960s.

“I set myself a significant task – to return Ukrainian literature to its humanistic image within the framework of world humanism.”
— V. Shevchuk (Interview for Ukrainian Literary Newspaper , 2014)

Shevchuk’s legacy is both a tribute to Zhytomyr and a testament to the resilience of Ukrainian cultural identity.

ABOUT THE WORK "A PATH IN THE GRASS. A ZHYTOMYR SAGA"

“I had a cult of the city Zhytomyr,” says Shevchuk, whose works are filled with references to his hometown.

A Path in the Grass. A Zhytomyr Saga portrays the lives of various social strata in Zhytomyr during the 1950s and 60s. Shevchuk masterfully examines the psychological struggles of his characters—intellectuals, workers, and officials—each searching for purpose and harmony in a world of loneliness and spiritual emptiness.

The storyline “Biletski” explores the life of an intelligent Ukrainian-Polish family, reflecting Shevchuk's own intercultural heritage, which Valeriy introduces the reader to in his autobiographical notes. At home, both Polish and Ukrainian literature were cherished, from Kraszewskyi and Marcinkowskyi to Nechuy-Levytskyi and Panas Myrny.

The Biletski family—a music teacher, a former actress, an oppressed poet, and a future pianist—faces personal trials that test their strength and humanity. Yet, Shevchuk’s philosophy ensures his characters hold onto life’s meaning, even in adversity.

The Boulevard serves as a symbolic thread throughout the saga, representing the journey toward a brighter, more fulfilled life.
